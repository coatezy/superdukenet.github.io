if you had the rear wheel off for a sprocket change at the same time i'll bet dollars to donuts the moron who put the wheel back in screwed it up, get it on a stand and check for side play while installed and try and turn it, does it turn freely? any strange noises or rubbing/grinding feeling through your hand as you turn it? i set my adjuster length by using a steel rule against the front edge of the slot where the adjusting thread winds into the swingarm against the front of the adjusting block, that's much more accurate than using the cast in "guides" which are usually way off. if all that is ok take the wheel out and check the wheel bearings, all three so remove the sprocket carrier, over torqueing the rear axle can dislodge a wheel bearing race, any notchiness or play and they need to be replaced.

This may or may not be suspension related. Op stated that he had new tyres installed as well.

Few questions:

Do the wobbles start when you encounter bumps in the road or are they present when the road surface is smooth?

What kind of tyres did you have mounted up? What pressures are you running?

How much do you weigh?

Stock bike? What year, model?

A slight misalignment in the rear will not cause a wobble. It will cause the bike to turn quicker in one direction than the other. you'll also wear out your chain and sprockets faster. To align rear do as a previous poster and I do, measure the distance from the surface which the locknut torques up against to the leading edge of the chain adjuster block. This is the only acurate way to set rear wheel alignment other than measuring from the swing arm pivot to the axel.
