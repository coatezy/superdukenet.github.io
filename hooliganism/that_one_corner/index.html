Zenbiker

2011-02-24 01:49:00 UTC

"off camber, hidden apex, downhill".

Hard pressed to think of a more difficult condition to ride smoothly.

The answer is : SLOW. Everything is working against you; the downhill is loading the front wheel, the off camber is challenging your cornering angle (you'll reach the limits of traction before you reach the limits of lean angle). Hidden apex: can't see it, cant set up for it.

On unfamiliar roads, caution is the name of the game. I have a similar corner on one of my fave roads, and here's the drill: Brake early to set your suspension and never, never, never go to trailing throttle. If you need to scrub speed, use your front brake but don't roll off the throttle. Stability of the chassis is the name of the game. Roll in, maintain throttle and use your front brake to control your speed (this is an old Freddie Spencer/Kevin Schwantz trick). Once you have the apex in site, feather off the brake while gentle feathering on the throttle; once past apex, slowly feed throttle and get the bike upright as soon as possible before you really begin to deliver power.

This is one of the most challenging conditions you will encounter; if you tend to drift to the opposite lane, you are NOT in control. These corners feel painfully slow, but if you can detach your mind and observe your bike position, you will see you are likely in a rather radical postition usually reserved for extreme lean angles on fast sweepers.

Of course, the more you run the corner, the more you will be familiar with THAT PARTICULAR CORNER. Don't try to translate it into similar corners. Road surface, road conditons, and subtle differences make each different. If you really want proof that God exists, take one of these corners with a bump just before the apex - unless your suspension is dialed in perfectly, His Holiness Jesus Christ will make an appearance before you.

Slow is controlled; controlled is smooth; smooth is fast. Practice, practice, practice.
