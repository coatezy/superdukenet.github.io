I'm a physical therapist working in the outpatient orthopaedic setting and work with many people who have back pain for a variety of reasons.

Ironic enough, I am loathe to give out advice to anybody who I haven't personally evaluated for the same reason it's hard to get solid advice from non-medical folks. Without knowledgeable hands/eyes/ears to see you as the big picture, it's impossible to formulate a specific (and thus effective) treatment regimen. Generalized approaches like yoga/pilates/self-help books/youtube( ) etc may help but are often not specific enough and may even make things worse.

If you've gotten x-rays and MRI to the area of complaint but haven't gotten solid answers, maybe seek a second opinion. My general experience with diagnostic test results is that they are often only a piece of the puzzle and may not be relevant at all! I always tell clients that x-ray and MRI tech is becoming so refined that they will pick up all but the smallest of details and the well-trained radiologist interpretting the results will objectively report those results as they see it. The missing piece is often the clinical relevance. Degeneration found? So what! Everybody (seriously) has degeneration occurring from the moment we stop growing to the day we die. People can be pain-free and active through all this (as you once were).

If you haven't tried PT yet, speak with you doctor about it. A good PT should be able to help you by trying different approaches to treat your pain and help set you on the road to staying pain-free. If progress is limited, a good PT should also be able to point you in a direction to pursue further help based on what did/did not work.

If you have tried everything, still haven't improved, or the pain is getting worse, you should probably revisit your doctor as this could be a sign of something more insidious. not to freak ya out tho the vast majority of people I've worked with improve greatly with conservative treatment.
