If you have a long working relationship with your tuner, then go with what they suggest.

Before you do however, here's the tradeoff - which with a little searching, you would have discovered:

- TuneECU requires nothing to implement other than an affordable cable. The big hit in cost with TuneECU is in time (labour and on the dyno). ANYONE - please correct me if this has changed - TuneECU is iterative in that you can't adjust the ECU's maps dynamically while the bike is on the dyno (and running). After each map change, the ECU has to be reset - which is a what - 15 minute process, or something like that each time. A tuner well-experienced with KTM twins, will be able to save you some dosh in that they should have a library of maps already, one or two of which are going something close (if not identical) to your setup. They then install this as a baseline, and fine-tune from there. There is also the matter of a given tuner's willingness to use TuneECU, but that hurdle you seem to have overcome.

- PCV (which should work on your bike, not a "full fat SDR" - but don't take my word for it - your tuner should know with certainty what will work on your bike), regardless what DynoJet states - works perfectly on my '08 SD. The downside is that you shell out for a black box, add wiring (OMFG- and a few grams weight), etc. The upsides are several. First - ANY decent tuner, whether experienced with KTM or not, can produce a decent tune for your setup. Yes, it supports indvidual maps for each cylinder. I believe it also can store 2 set s of maps - say for tunes with and w/o baffles in the cans. They don't need a library (DynoJet provides), and they can tune dynamically - literally on-the-fly. This usually costs <1/2 to tune vs. TuneECU (over here anyway). So every time you do a mod that benefits from a tune - this can save $$. In other words, only a couple of tuning sessions in, and the PCV is net cost the same as TuneECU.

Additional features of the PCV is that it only needs a standard USB cable and DynoJet's application (free), if you want to muck with the maps yourself - same access as TuneECU in this regard. For some, the real-time tuning of the optional Autotune product (AT-300 for SDs - exploits dual O2 sensors), can ensure optimization at all times, for varying conditions of load, altitude etc. Maybe not that important in the UK, but of value here where a ride from sea level to >2K meters is an hour or less in each direction. Many of our tracks in the region (most are inland) are at substantially greater elevation than the coast (sea level). No muss, no fuss. Also, when minor mods are made, the Autotune can automatically adjust for these (e.g. change in air filter, or the above noted removal of exhaust baffles for track use), without necessitating a full re-tune.

I am not that strongly biased by my own choice, but a PCV is relatively cheap (< a set of decent tyres here - and will last much longer - lol). The ease of use + flexibility in features were what ultimately sold me; this was after I had been using TuneECU.
